I am currently a Ph.D. candidate and teaching assistant in Econometrics & Quantitative Economics at American University, with an expected graduation date of May 2025. My research interests include macroeconomics, monetary policy, international finance, financial stability, and climate economics. I also hold a Master of Public Administration from Columbia University's School of International and Public Affairs, where I studied under renowned economist Guillermo Calvo.

With years of experience at the World Bank and universities in the U.S., I am currently a consultant at the World Bank Group, where I lead research on the macroeconomic impacts of climate change and develop assessments that integrate financial stability and policy analysis. Additionally, I am involved in the China Economic Policy Uncertainty (CPU) Project, where I manage data collection and analysis to contribute to our understanding of economic policy uncertainty in China.

Previously, I worked as a core member of a four-economist team contributing to the World Bank's semi-annual Macro-Financial Review, where I played a key role in the development and maintenance of the Macro-Financial Spider Tool Model, used to assess global macro-financial risks. This model has been employed in internal World Bank reports and meetings with the Financial Stability Board. My work effectively bridges the gap between academic research and practical policy-making.

In addition to my professional experience, I have co-authored papers published in international conferences, including the International Conference on Management Science and Engineering Management, and have contributed to several internal reports for the World Bank Group. My academic achievements have been recognized with the Frank M. Tamagna Prize for excellence in international finance and banking, and I have represented millennial voices at the United Nations Global Summit.

Proficient in Python, MATLAB, Dynare, R, STATA, and other research tools
